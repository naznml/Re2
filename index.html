Мәтін Текст

B1+ Reading Practice · University Life

Topic: The University of Life

The majority of eighteen-year-old students entering higher education go straight from school to university. For many school leavers, however, there is the irresistible attraction of the “gap year”, a time between school and university when they decide to experience something new, different or exciting.

Many of these so-called “gappers” go travelling around the world, often supplementing their limited funds by taking on casual work. Others may do voluntary work in a village in a distant part of the world. For the majority of gappers, the gap year is simply a chance to enjoy life as an independent adult for the first time.

Increasingly, however, gap years are also proving a useful way of renewing a lapsed or weakening interest in education. They offer young people a chance to think about why they should study, or whether they need to study at all. A growing number of students, having taken a break after school, are heading back into further and higher education through a less direct route of working and gapping.

According to recent data from the British university admissions service, UCAS, 105,000 students aged nineteen and 44,400 students aged twenty entered higher education last year. These figures show a steady annual increase in this age group over the previous three years.

Nineteen-year-old student Christine Samways is a typical example. She left school at sixteen with nine good exam passes at grades A to C, but she did not want to continue studying at the time. She was also worried that, despite having the qualities of a good student, she would find the challenges of higher education too great and would be forced to drop out.

Instead, Christine gained a vocational qualification in hairdressing. However, she quickly began to realise that it was not quite what she wanted and that going back into some kind of education could be her next step. Like many sixteen-year-old school leavers starting work for the first time, she understood that without qualifications, or without the right qualifications, people have fewer work choices.

“The things that you want to do just are not available to you,” she says. Her experience shows that a break from formal study does not always mean turning away from education. For some young people, time spent working, travelling or volunteering can help them return to study with a clearer sense of purpose.

Мәтін Текст

B1+ Reading Practice · Scientific Work

Topic: The Scientific Method

Hypotheses are often described as imaginative and inspiring. They are “adventures of the mind” because they allow researchers to think beyond what is already known. Some philosophers of science have argued that the scientific method is not simply inductive, as is commonly believed, but hypothetico-deductive.

As a researcher, it is important to understand the difference between these two descriptions of the research process. Research does not always begin with a perfectly neutral observation of facts. Therefore, researchers should not feel that they are “cheating” if their work begins with an idea, expectation or possible explanation.

The common myth of the scientific method is that it is purely inductive. According to this view, scientific theory begins with simple, unbiased and impartial observation. Researchers collect raw sensory information, usually called facts, and then general theories somehow develop from this collection of information. However, this starting point is problematic.

There is no such thing as completely impartial observation. Every action we observe is influenced by what we have already seen, learned or experienced in the past. Scientific work of an experimental or research nature usually begins with certain expectations about the result. This expectation is called a hypothesis.

Hypotheses give direction and energy to investigation. They affect the methodology because, in the light of an expectation, some observations are considered relevant while others are seen as unrelated. One method is selected and another is rejected. Some experiments are carried out, while others are not. In this sense, the idea of a completely innocent, pure and objective researcher is difficult to defend.

Hypotheses may arise through conjecture or inspiration, but they must be formalized and tested carefully using a suitable method. If the predictions made from a hypothesis are not shown to be correct, the hypothesis should be changed or rejected. If the predictions are accurate, the hypothesis may be supported and kept until further tests show that it needs to be revised.

Мәтін Текст

B2 Reading Practice · Scientists and Society

Topic: Science and Human Life

Science and the techniques to which it has given rise have changed human life during the last hundred and fifty years more than it had been changed since human beings first took to agriculture. The changes brought about by science continue at increasing speed, and there is no sign that a new stable scientific plateau will soon be reached.

On the contrary, there is every reason to think that the revolutionary possibilities of science extend immeasurably beyond what has so far been realised. This raises an important question: can the human race adjust itself quickly enough to these rapid transformations, or will it, like many former species, perish from lack of adaptability?

The dinosaurs were once the lords of creation. If there had been philosophers among them, not one would have foreseen that the whole race might perish. Yet they became extinct because they could not adapt themselves to a world without swamps.

In the case of human beings and science, there is a completely new factor. Human beings themselves are creating the changes of environment to which they will have to adjust with unprecedented rapidity. Although human scientific skill is the cause of these environmental changes, most of them are not directly intended by human beings.

Even when changes come about through human actions, they can have something of the inevitability of natural forces. Whether nature dries up swamps or people deliberately drain them makes little difference to the final result. In both cases, living creatures must adapt or face serious consequences.

Whether human beings will be able to survive the changes of environment that their own skill has brought about remains an open question. If the answer is positive, people will have to apply scientific ways of thinking not only to nature, but also to themselves and to their social institutions.

Reading strategy

  • Main idea (негізгі ой) сұрағында science (ғылым), human life (адам өмірі) және adaptation (бейімделу) арасындағы байланысты тап.
  • Purpose question (мақсат сұрағы) үшін author (автор) мысалды не үшін келтіргенін ойла: dinosaurs example adaptation идеясын түсіндіреді.
  • Inference (қорытынды жасау) сұрағында тура сөйлемді емес, logical meaning (логикалық мағына)-ны таңда.
  • Cause-effect (себеп-салдар байланысы) үшін because, due to, whether, result, adjust сияқты signal words (белгі сөздер)-ге қара.
  • В main idea (главная мысль) найди связь между science (наукой), human life (жизнью человека) и adaptation (адаптацией).
  • В purpose question (вопрос на цель) подумай, зачем author (автор) использует пример: dinosaurs example объясняет adaptation.
  • В inference (вывод) выбирай не прямую фразу, а logical meaning (логический смысл).
  • Для cause-effect (причинно-следственная связь) отмечай signal words (сигнальные слова): because, due to, whether, result, adjust.
